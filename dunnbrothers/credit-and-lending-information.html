Credit and Lending Information

Credit reporting is a system lenders use to decide whether or not to give you credit or a loan and how much interest they can charge you for it . Credit is a historical record of debts and payments. There are three credit agencies: Experian, TransUnion and Equifax. It is good to know your score for all three prior to working with a lender since each may contain different information. Don’t panic if you have any negative marks. The Fair Credit Reporting Act allows consumers to review and contest any item on the report. Also, a trend of on-time payments can help with earning a lenders trust and qualifying for a new loan.

Credit is held for 10 years and is more or less a financial resume. Your individual report created from a number of sources including but not limited to the IRS, department stores, gasoline companies, or anyone that lends money. Be sure to keep in mind pulling your credit report numerous times will have a negative effect on your score. However, you can receive all three reports once a year free of charge with no negative effects from Annual Credit Report After gathering a number of documents to determine your income, payment history, amount of debt, length and type of credit a lender will input information to determine your FICO score which is based on a mathematical formula. These scores range from 300 (Bad) to 850 (good). This score is based both on positive and negative information on your credit. Your FICO scored is weighted with the following: Payment history (35%), Amounts owed (30%), Length of credit history (15%), New credit (10%), Types of credit (10%).

The rule of thumb is the higher FICO score the less risk to the lender. In addition, lenders will use front-end, back-end, debt-income and a number of other ratios to determine risk. If you’re trying to re- establish credit it is difficult but far from impossible. Things like foreclosure, bankruptcy, missed or late payments are not the end of the world. However, be sure it is not a reoccurring theme. Three years of strong credit following this sort of negative information is usually enough for most lenders to reconsider ability to pay off debt.
