Mortgages

With new additional restriction in lending due to the many subprime loans it has become increasingly more difficult to get approved for a loan. Fortunately, interest rates are nearing historically low interest rates. This makes owning a home easier now than ever. However, with a loan there is more cost than just the interest alone.

Points are often associated with the cost of lending money. A point is a form of prepaid interest and translates mathematically to one percent. Lenders may charge you points upon the close to cover discount points and lender fees. Another option is the ability to pay discount points in exchange for lowering your monthly payment. Lenders may also charge points based on poor ratios or credit worthiness.

Getting preapproved or prequalified improves your chances of having your offer accepted because it shows that you are a serious buyer. During this time a lender will collect a number of documents such as paystubs, W-2’s, 1040 Tax Returns, bank statements, and identification like a driver’s license and social security card. After analyzing these documents and scrutinizing your credit and debt information they will provide you with a written letter on an approved loan amount.

If you fit certain criteria you can qualify for specialty loans. For instance, first time home buyers have several programs that offer loan assistant options. One of these is an FHA loan. These sorts of loans are government backed loans that are designed to help qualified buyers who can’t afford a down payment. This loan is extremely popular and provides up to 97% financing but comes with the cost of premium mortgage insurance and strict credit requirements. This is just one of many programs that lenders have to offer so don’t hold back and think that you won’t be able to qualify. In addition to first time buyer programs, there are also veteran loans known as VA loans that can offer low to no-down payment options. We can help determine which loan best fits your financials needs whether it is a local, state, federal or standard conventional loan.
